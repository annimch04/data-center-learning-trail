# Missouri Data Center Site-Selection Rubric

Status: First public draft  
Created: 2026-06-06  
Learning Trail item: Site Selection

## Purpose

This artifact is a first-pass rubric for evaluating which Missouri regions could plausibly support data center development while balancing technical feasibility, public infrastructure, economic development, and community benefit.

The rubric compares site archetypes, not exact parcels. The goal is to create a practical research framework before making site-specific claims.

## Core Question

What would make Missouri a serious data center target, and where would the public benefit case actually be strongest?

## Working Hypothesis

Missouri may become interesting for AI and data center infrastructure when a site can combine power readiness, transmission feasibility, fiber access, physical resilience, permitting capacity, and a clear public benefit case. The strongest sites may not be the places with the most incentives. They may be places where infrastructure investment also solves regional constraints, such as broadband gaps, workforce development needs, or underused industrial assets.

## Site Archetypes

### 1. Springfield / Branson Underground Infrastructure Corridor

This archetype tests whether existing underground infrastructure, geology, security, temperature stability, and regional fiber/power assets could support specialized data center use.

Early questions:

- What parts of the underground corridor are actually suitable for modern data center loads?
- Does underground siting reduce cooling, security, or resilience costs enough to matter?
- Are power and fiber assets strong enough for hyperscale, edge, or specialized colocation use?
- What local concerns would emerge around water, traffic, energy rates, and emergency access?

### 2. Southeast Missouri Rural Network-Deficit Region

This archetype tests whether data center-adjacent infrastructure could create mutual benefit in a region where rural broadband and economic development needs remain important.

Early questions:

- Could a data center project accelerate fiber, power, or workforce investment that also benefits local communities?
- Would water, flood risk, power deliverability, or transmission constraints make the public benefit case weak?
- Are economic development incentives justified if permanent job counts are limited?
- Which community anchor institutions could benefit from improved connectivity?

### 3. Northern Missouri Rural Connectivity Corridor

This archetype tests rural corridor development where land availability may be stronger but transmission, workforce, and broadband constraints may be decisive.

Early questions:

- Are there transmission corridors, substations, cooperatives, or broadband projects that create a viable backbone?
- Would a project strengthen regional infrastructure or mostly bypass local needs?
- What local government capacity exists for evaluating complex utility, tax, and environmental tradeoffs?
- Could smaller edge or modular deployments make more sense than hyperscale sites?

### 4. Kansas City / St. Louis Edge-Adjacent Metro Infrastructure Zone

This archetype tests whether metro-adjacent areas are more realistic because they may offer stronger fiber, workforce, utility planning, permitting capacity, and logistics.

Early questions:

- Does metro adjacency solve enough fiber, power, and workforce issues to outweigh land and community constraints?
- How should ratepayer protection, infrastructure cost allocation, and tax incentives be evaluated?
- Are there industrial sites where redevelopment could produce public value?
- Would edge compute, enterprise colocation, or AI inference workloads fit better than large training clusters?

## Scoring Scale

Use a 1-5 score for each category.

- `1`: Major constraint or poor fit
- `2`: Weak fit; significant unanswered questions
- `3`: Plausible but not yet proven
- `4`: Strong candidate with manageable constraints
- `5`: Excellent fit with strong evidence

Add an evidence confidence rating beside each score:

- `Low`: mostly hypothesis or unsourced public discussion
- `Medium`: supported by public documents, but not site-specific enough
- `High`: supported by direct public data, operator confirmation, or field interviews

## Short Memo: First Take

Missouri becomes a serious data center target if it can show that large-load power service, transmission deliverability, fiber redundancy, water/cooling feasibility, and local permitting can be coordinated without shifting unreasonable costs or risks onto residents and small businesses. The public benefit case is strongest where data center development is tied to durable infrastructure improvements: stronger grid assets, better broadband, reuse of industrial or underground sites, workforce pathways, and transparent local agreements.

The strongest early research contrast is not simply urban versus rural. It is whether a site creates shared infrastructure value or merely consumes scarce infrastructure. Metro-adjacent zones may score higher on fiber, workforce, permitting capacity, and utility readiness. Underground corridors may have distinctive physical resilience and security advantages, but still need proof around power scale, cooling design, fiber redundancy, and emergency operations. Rural deficit regions may offer the most compelling public benefit story if investment also improves broadband and regional infrastructure, but they may face the hardest constraints around transmission, workforce, water, and local evaluation capacity.

The key question for each archetype is therefore not "Could a data center be built here?" but "What would have to be true for this project to improve the regional system around it?"

## Field-Learning Plan

### Visits

- Springfield Underground / Bluebird Underground area, if a tour or conversation is possible
- Branson-area underground infrastructure, if operator access is available
- One southeast Missouri community with broadband or economic development needs
- One northern Missouri rural corridor community
- One KC or St. Louis metro-adjacent industrial or redevelopment zone

### Conversations

- Underground infrastructure operators
- Electric utilities, rural electric cooperatives, and municipal utilities
- Missouri Public Service Commission or utility regulatory observers
- Regional transmission planning experts familiar with MISO/SPP
- Economic development organizations
- Local government officials and planners
- Rural broadband stakeholders and co-ops
- Water and wastewater utility operators
- Workforce boards, community colleges, and technical education providers
- Community advocates or local journalists tracking data center proposals

### Interview Questions

- What is the first constraint you would check before taking a data center proposal seriously?
- What infrastructure would the project need to pay for itself?
- What risks could be shifted onto residents if the agreement is weak?
- What public benefits would make the project worth considering?
- What information is usually missing when these proposals first appear?
- What would you want included in a transparent community review process?

## Research Backlog

- Identify Missouri utility territories and which RTO/ISO planning regions matter for each archetype.
- Locate public data center tariffs, large-load rules, or PSC materials relevant to cost allocation.
- Build a source list for Missouri broadband gaps and BEAD-funded projects.
- Compare state and local incentive tools for data center projects.
- Gather flood, water stress, and cooling-relevant data by region.
- Identify existing underground facilities, current tenants, and technical constraints.
- Draft a one-page scoring worksheet that can be reused for each archetype.

## Public Boundary

This artifact is an exploratory learning product, not an investment recommendation or site certification. Any future scored version should cite sources, separate facts from hypotheses, and disclose confidence levels.
