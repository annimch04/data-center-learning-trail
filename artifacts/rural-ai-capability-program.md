# Rural AI Capability Program

Status: Draft concept  
Created: 2026-06-07  
Learning Trail topic: Community infrastructure, workforce development, and AI adoption

## Purpose

This artifact explores how rural communities could build local AI capability before, during, or entirely apart from major AI infrastructure development.

The core idea is that AI infrastructure is not only a compute, power, fiber, water, or data center question. It is also a local capability question. A community that receives AI infrastructure but lacks the ability to evaluate, govern, implement, and adapt AI remains dependent on outside vendors, consultants, and opaque technical claims. A community that develops practical AI capability gains durable economic and institutional capacity regardless of whether it ever hosts a major data center.

## Core Question

What capability should exist in a rural community before major AI infrastructure arrives?

## Working Hypothesis

Rural municipalities need practical AI literacy and governance programs more urgently than they need direct access to advanced models.

Communities that understand AI procurement, governance, implementation, privacy, workforce development, and infrastructure planning will create greater long-term value than communities focused only on acquiring tools, attracting facilities, or reacting to vendor proposals.

## Relationship To Data Centers

Data centers are one component of a broader AI infrastructure ecosystem, but they are not the center of this model.

The Missouri site-selection rubric asks where AI/data center infrastructure could create mutual benefit instead of simply consuming land, power, water, fiber, tax capacity, or public attention.

This artifact asks the companion question: what local competence would allow a community to negotiate, govern, benefit from, or decline AI-related infrastructure from a position of understanding?

Infrastructure without capability creates dependency. Infrastructure combined with capability can create resilience.

## Problem Statement

Most public AI conversations focus on frontier models, startups, enterprise software, research labs, and consumer tools.

Less attention is given to the institutions that will still face AI decisions:

- City governments
- County governments
- Rural economic development organizations
- Planning commissions
- Utility districts
- Workforce boards
- Public libraries
- Community colleges
- Regional planning commissions

These organizations will increasingly encounter practical AI decisions even when they lack internal technical capacity.

Questions they will face include:

- Which AI vendors should be trusted?
- What data should remain local?
- What public records considerations apply?
- How should employees use AI?
- What workforce skills should be prioritized?
- What infrastructure investments make sense?
- How can a community avoid dependency on a single vendor?
- How should local leaders evaluate claims made by AI companies?

The challenge is not only access to AI. The challenge is capability.

## The AI Extension Service Model

This concept borrows from the cooperative extension model: a public-interest system for translating technical knowledge into practical local capacity.

Traditional extension systems helped communities evaluate and adapt technologies and practices related to agriculture, soil, irrigation, equipment, community development, and business needs.

An AI extension model would help communities evaluate and adapt:

- AI systems
- AI vendors
- AI workforce needs
- AI infrastructure investments
- Data governance practices
- Public-sector use cases
- Procurement and contract risks
- Local policy and operational practices

The extension model emphasizes local knowledge, practical training, community-specific adaptation, and long-term capability building instead of one-time technology deployments.

## Curriculum Framework

### Module 1: AI Foundations For Local Government

Purpose:
Provide practical understanding of AI capabilities and limitations.

Topics:

- What AI is and is not
- Generative AI versus traditional software
- Common AI misconceptions
- Current capabilities and limitations
- Public-sector use cases
- Where human judgment remains essential

Outcome:
Participants can discuss AI without relying on vendor marketing.

### Module 2: AI Procurement And Vendor Evaluation

Purpose:
Help municipalities avoid poor purchasing decisions and long-term lock-in.

Topics:

- Evaluating vendor claims
- Questions every municipality should ask
- Data ownership
- Contract terms and exit planning
- Lock-in risks
- Security and support expectations
- Pilot design before full procurement

Outcome:
Participants can conduct more informed vendor reviews.

### Module 3: Data Governance, Privacy, And Public Records

Purpose:
Develop responsible practices for public-sector AI use.

Topics:

- Public records
- Citizen data
- Sensitive information
- Retention policies
- Privacy considerations
- Local versus cloud storage
- Cybersecurity basics
- Staff usage policies

Outcome:
Participants understand governance implications before implementation.

### Module 4: Municipal AI Use Cases

Purpose:
Identify realistic opportunities for productivity gains without overpromising transformation.

Potential use cases:

- Grant writing support
- Policy drafting
- Meeting summaries
- Public communication
- Economic development research
- Permitting assistance
- Workforce analysis
- Internal knowledge management

Outcome:
Participants identify high-value pilot projects with low public risk.

### Module 5: Workforce Development

Purpose:
Prepare communities for workforce shifts and new skill needs.

Topics:

- AI literacy pathways
- Community college partnerships
- Technical education
- Apprenticeship models
- Local business adoption
- Workforce transition planning
- Train-the-trainer models

Outcome:
Communities develop local talent strategies rather than outsourcing all AI competence.

### Module 6: Infrastructure Awareness

Purpose:
Connect AI adoption to physical infrastructure realities.

Topics:

- Data centers
- Power requirements
- Broadband and middle-mile infrastructure
- Edge computing
- Local versus centralized compute
- Cooling and water implications
- Resilience and emergency operations

Outcome:
Participants understand how AI adoption connects to land, power, water, broadband, and resilience.

### Module 7: Economic Development Strategy

Purpose:
Explore how AI affects regional competitiveness.

Topics:

- Entrepreneurship
- Remote work
- Local business adoption
- Infrastructure attraction
- Workforce positioning
- Public benefit evaluation
- Negotiating from local priorities

Outcome:
Communities integrate AI into broader development planning.

## Pilot Program Sketch

### Pilot Site

Start with one Missouri municipality, county, or regional cohort that has:

- Active economic development questions
- Broadband or infrastructure needs
- Local government willingness to participate
- A community college, library, extension, or workforce partner
- At least one realistic AI use case that does not require sensitive data

### Pilot Duration

Eight to twelve weeks.

### Pilot Outputs

- AI readiness self-assessment
- Vendor evaluation checklist
- Public-sector AI use policy starter
- Data governance checklist
- One low-risk internal AI pilot
- Workforce and training map
- Infrastructure awareness memo
- Public benefit questions for AI/data center proposals

### Success Metrics

- Participants can explain AI capabilities and limits in plain language.
- Local officials can evaluate vendor claims with a standard checklist.
- The community has a draft AI use policy or governance memo.
- At least one low-risk pilot is tested and documented.
- Workforce and infrastructure implications are mapped.
- Public-facing decisions include clearer questions about benefit, risk, and accountability.
